The Design Challenge

Build systems that worked technically and served the intended player experience

Gameplay programming often sits close to design because small implementation choices can dramatically change how a feature feels. My challenge was to build and support gameplay systems that were technically reliable while also supporting level flow, progression, combat feel and player clarity.

Checkpoint flow

How should a checkpoint system support level flow and progression without interrupting challenge or pacing?

Multiplayer clarity

How do multiplayer systems support co-op, matchmaking and combat design while clearly communicating state to players?

Moment-to-moment feel

How do weapons, vendors, characters and core systems support responsive, readable gameplay?

Design Decisions

Principles that shaped the work

01 - Support flow
Checkpoints should support progression, not interrupt it.

The goal was to reduce frustration while preserving challenge, pacing and level readability.

02 - Communicate state
Multiplayer systems need clarity and reliability.

Co-op and matchmaking features need to work consistently and communicate state clearly to players.

03 - Start with impact
Technical design starts with player experience.

Even when writing code, I tried to stay aware of how implementation choices would affect feel, feedback and usability.

Implementation & Collaboration

Close collaboration across design, engineering and QA

Technical communication

This role required clear communication with design, engineering, QA and other disciplines. I learned how to explain technical details, support design goals through implementation and identify when a feature needed more design clarity before it could be built well.

Design awareness

Working close to implementation taught me to think about how systems behave in real gameplay, not just how they look in documentation. That awareness now helps me design features that are more practical, testable and production-ready.

Lessons Learned

Key takeaways

What this strengthened

  • Technical confidence in design conversations.
  • Understanding of how systems are built, tested and maintained.
  • Ability to communicate clearly with engineers and QA.
  • Awareness of constraints, edge cases and production realities.

How it shaped my design style

This experience became the foundation for my current design style. It taught me how to think technically, communicate with engineers, understand constraints and design features that are more likely to survive contact with production.

It also gave me credibility in technical design conversations because I understand how systems are built, not just how they are imagined.
