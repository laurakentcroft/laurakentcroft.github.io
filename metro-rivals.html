Summary

On Metro Rivals, I worked across gameplay, systems and technical design while taking on Lead Designer responsibilities. I owned features from concept through documentation, implementation, balancing and iteration, including gameplay systems, economy, LiveOps, vehicles, movement, narrative and player-facing progression.

I also supported junior and mid-level designers, acted as a key point of contact for design decisions, and worked closely with production, UI, art, engineering, QA and localisation to keep features scoped, readable and aligned with the intended player experience.

Challenge

Designing clear, scalable features within real production constraints

The main challenge was designing and delivering gameplay and systems features that felt clear, fair and engaging for a broad player base, while also staying achievable within production and technical constraints.

This meant balancing player fantasy, readability, implementation effort, stakeholder needs and long-term system health.

Key design questions

  • How do we make vehicle handling feel responsive, accessible and readable?
  • How do we design economy and LiveOps systems that support motivation without overwhelming the experience?
  • How do we keep features scoped while preserving the intended player fantasy?
  • How do we communicate design intent clearly enough that all disciplines can move in the same direction?

Design Decisions

The principles behind the work

01 - Prioritising readability
Make systems understandable before making them complex.

I focused on how features were structured, how values were balanced, how UI information was surfaced and how feedback was communicated.

02 - Designing for scalability
Build systems that can support future content.

For economy, LiveOps and progression systems, I considered future events, customisation, rewards and player motivation.

03 - Balancing creativity with feasibility
Keep ideas exciting, buildable and maintainable.

I aimed to preserve the intended player fantasy while keeping features practical for teams to implement, test and iterate.

04 - Supporting broad skill levels
Make gameplay accessible while preserving skill expression.

For vehicles and gameplay values, I considered accessibility, fairness, readability and the intended simcade feel.

1

Define the player experience

Clarified what the player should understand, what decisions they should make, what feedback they needed and how the system connected to the wider game loop.

2

Break the feature into systems

Mapped how the feature connected to progression, economy, UI, vehicles, rewards, balancing, LiveOps or narrative depending on the feature.

3

Document the design

Created structured GDDs and TDDs explaining feature goals, rules, values, edge cases, dependencies and implementation considerations.

4

Align with stakeholders

Presented feature requests and documentation to stakeholders and directors, then worked with cross-discipline teams to clarify scope and execution.

5

Implement, test and iterate

Where relevant, implemented or tuned values directly in Unreal Engine 5, reviewed how the feature felt in practice and adjusted based on feedback.

Collaboration

Bridging design intent and final player experience

Implementation & collaboration

I worked closely with engineering and other disciplines to make sure features were not only well designed, but buildable. I used documentation, wireframes, conversations, reviews and implementation support to help bridge the gap between design intent and final player experience.

Translating between disciplines

My role often involved explaining the player-facing goal to technical teams, clarifying constraints back to design and production, and adjusting features when feedback or scope required it.

Outcome

Key takeaways

What this project strengthened

  • Owning complex player-facing features end to end.
  • Communicating design intent clearly across disciplines.
  • Balancing player fantasy with production and technical constraints.
  • Supporting other designers through reviews, scheduling and mentoring.

What I learned

This project strengthened my confidence as a designer who can own complex features, support other designers and communicate across disciplines. It also reinforced that strong design leadership is not about having every answer immediately. It is about keeping the team aligned, asking the right questions, making decisions when needed and creating space for people to do their best work.
