Context

Why this sample exists

Confidentiality note

This is an original, anonymised design sample created for portfolio purposes. It is informed by professional quest, systems and narrative design experience, but does not reproduce shipped content, internal documentation, confidential project details or final design work from any specific project.

Design challenge

Create quest-based content that supports player motivation, progression and replayable engagement while remaining practical to implement, test, localise and maintain.

The key problem is balancing player expression with production scope: giving players meaningful choices without creating an unmanageable amount of unique content for every branch.

Contribution

What I would own or define

Quest and narrative design

  • Quest objectives, pacing and progression requirements
  • Quest-giver characters with distinct motivations and end goals
  • Dialogue choices that express player intent and roleplay style
  • Branching choices, follow-up states and player-facing consequences
  • Player motivations such as heroism, revenge, fame, curiosity and reward-seeking

Implementation and delivery

  • XP, rewards, unlocks and completion requirements
  • Quest logic, state tracking, edge cases and fallback states
  • Required assets: characters, VO, dialogue strings, UI prompts and reward data
  • Localisation notes, string context and tone guidance
  • Clear documentation for design, engineering, UI, art, audio, QA and production

Design reasoning

I use this type of structure to separate the mechanical quest path from the narrative expression layer. The core objective remains stable enough to implement, balance and test reliably, while dialogue choices allow the player to express why they are taking part.

This helps avoid uncontrolled scope expansion while still giving players a sense of agency. The player may complete the same core objective, but the tone, NPC response, reward framing or later follow-up dialogue can change based on whether they acted out of heroism, revenge, fame or reward-seeking.

Design reasoning

I document quests in this level of detail because quest design sits between multiple disciplines. A quest is not only a written idea; it needs to become playable content with clear implementation rules, asset requirements, UI needs, reward logic, localisation context and testable edge cases.

03

Character motivation and dialogue choice

The player encounters an NPC who asks for help with a task that appears community-focused. On the surface, the NPC frames the request as beneficial to everyone. Underneath, they want status, recognition and leverage over a rival.

CharacterSurface goalHidden / personal motiveDesign purpose
Quest giverWants help completing an urgent task.Wants recognition, fame and leverage over a rival.Creates tension between what the NPC says and what they really want.
PlayerCan choose why they engage.Heroism, revenge, fame, money or curiosity.Lets the player project motivation onto the quest instead of simply accepting an objective.
Rival NPCMay be affected by the outcome.Wants to protect their reputation or position.Creates room for later consequences or changed dialogue.
NPC: "You're exactly the person I was hoping to see. There's a problem, and if we solve it before anyone else notices, everyone benefits."
Player Choice A - Heroic
"If people need help, I'm in. Tell me what needs doing."

Frames the player as altruistic and community-minded. Later dialogue can praise their reliability or sense of duty.

Player Choice B - Reward-seeking
"That sounds like work. What do I get out of it?"

Lets the player express practical or self-interested motivation. The NPC becomes more transactional and foregrounds the reward.

Player Choice C - Fame / status
"If this makes me look good, I'm listening."

Supports players who want recognition or public success. Follow-up dialogue can reference reputation and visibility.

Player Choice D - Revenge / rivalry
"If this causes trouble for them, I'm interested."

Ties the player into existing conflict. The NPC can reveal more of their personal agenda and later dialogue can reference escalation.

Design reasoning

These choices let players express motivation without requiring a completely separate quest for every branch. The underlying objective can remain stable, while dialogue tone, NPC response, reward framing and later consequences change.

This creates a stronger sense of roleplay while keeping the quest achievable within production constraints. It also prevents quest-givers from feeling like simple objective dispensers: each NPC has a motive, an end goal, and a reason to involve the player.

Design reasoning

I think about implementation from the beginning so that quest design is not just a written concept. It needs to be trackable, testable and recoverable from edge cases.

This means considering quest state, progression requirements, branch variables, objective completion, disconnect recovery, reward timing, follow-up consequences, QA coverage, localisation context and tool or engineering support.
